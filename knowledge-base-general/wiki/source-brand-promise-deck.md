# Brand Promise deck

**What it is:** An 11-slide internal strategy deck defining Project Food's brand promise, the
four brand pillars (with explicit guardrail rules), a Maslow framing, the word-of-mouth model,
and the stickiness/moat thesis.
**Origin:** Project Food internal presentation.
**Raw file:** `raw/presentations/Brand promise-projectfood.pdf`
**Date:** publish date unknown; ingested 2026-05-30.

## Key takeaways
- The brand promise is **"Eat 30 different plants a week. Good gut, good life."** — specific,
  true, targeted, and consistent across every surface.
- **The number is the product.** A 0–30 count of unique plants per week; the app measures it
  directly. See [[concept-30-plants-a-week]].
- Four brand pillars govern product decisions, each with hard "we never" rules. See
  [[concept-brand-pillars]].
- Growth is designed through three "talkable moments," never through nagging. See
  [[concept-word-of-mouth]].
- The moat is recurring weekly utility, not the distant health payoff: *"without us, Sunday
  gets harder."* See [[concept-stickiness-moat]].
- Target user: people who believe food affects how they feel and want a simple, gamified way
  to act on it. See [[persona-believer]].
- Explicit contrast competitor: [[entity-myfitnesspal]] ("nobody pulls out MyFitnessPal at
  dinner").

## Notable quotes
> The number is the product.

> Each plant counts once. What you do with it is up to you.

> Tap as you eat. Count by the week.

> Good food, good week, good life. Never homework.

> The best marketing is a user pointing at their phone.

> Without us, Sunday gets harder.

## Maslow framing (slide 2)
The deck maps the product up Maslow's hierarchy: Physiological = *eat 30 different plants*;
Safety = *long-term gut health / microbiome diversity*; Belonging = *shared ritual ("we're
all chasing 30")*; Esteem = *streaks, leaderboard, the dopamine layer*; Self-actualization =
*good gut, good life*.

## Implications for Project Food
- Every surface (onboarding, empty states, re-engagement, notifications, emails) should point
  at "30 plants this week." The deck flags onboarding, empty states, and re-engagement as the
  places consistency usually breaks.
- The pillars' "we never" rules are effectively product guardrails — useful as an acceptance
  checklist when reviewing new features.
