# Research notes — origin of "30 plants a week"

**What it is:** Web-research notes establishing where the "30 different plants a week" idea
comes from and the evidence behind it. Collected 2026-05-30.

## Key facts
- The recommendation traces to the **American Gut Project** (a citizen-science study of
  >10,000 people across the US/UK/Australia; results published 2018). **Tim Spector** led the
  UK arm ("British Gut Project").
- Core finding: people who ate **30+ different plants per week** had **more diverse gut
  microbiomes** and more "good" gut bacteria than those eating ~10 — including putative
  short-chain-fatty-acid (SCFA) producers such as *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii* and
  *Oscillospira*. Plants supply varied fibers, resistant starches, polyphenols and prebiotics
  that feed a broader microbial community.
- Spector co-founded **ZOE** (personalized-nutrition company), which has popularized the
  "30 plants a week" message in the mainstream. See [[entity-zoe]], [[entity-tim-spector]].
- The idea is now broad public-health messaging (Waitrose, ProVeg, etc. all promote it), not a
  proprietary concept.

## Implications for Project Food
- The science behind the core mechanic ([[concept-30-plants-a-week]]) is real and defensible —
  good for "true" in the brand promise.
- But the **number is not ownable**: it's a widely promoted public guideline, which is why a
  crowd of apps use it (see [[source-competitor-scan]]). Differentiation has to come from
  *execution*, not the number.
